Frequency describes repetition rate in cycles per second, measured in hertz. Phase
describes position within a cycle. Filters and delays change the relationship between
frequency components, so frequency and phase must be understood together.

## Frequency and period

For a periodic signal:

```text
period in seconds = 1 / frequency in hertz
```

A 1 kHz sine completes one cycle every 1 ms. At 48 kHz sample rate, that cycle spans
48 sample intervals.

## A spectrum is a view, not another signal

An analyzer estimates how energy is distributed across frequency for a chosen time
window. Window length, window function, FFT size, overlap, and signal stationarity all
affect what you see.

Use a spectrum to test frequency-domain claims, but do not treat one frame as a full
description of a time-varying sound.

## Filter magnitude

A filter's magnitude response states how strongly it passes each frequency. Cutoff is
usually a transition reference, not a brick-wall boundary. Slope and resonance shape
that transition.

Different filter designs can share a nominal cutoff yet differ in:

- transition shape;
- resonance or Q behavior;
- phase response;
- modulation behavior;
- numerical behavior near extreme parameters; and
- nonlinear or self-oscillating behavior.

Use the block's exact reference and measurements rather than assuming all “low-pass”
filters are interchangeable.

## Delay creates frequency-dependent phase shift

A time delay shifts every component by the same amount in seconds. Expressed as phase,
that shift grows with frequency:

```text
phase shift = -2π × frequency × delay
```

When a delayed signal is mixed with its undelayed version, some frequencies reinforce
and others cancel. The result is a comb-like magnitude response.

This is the foundation of flanging and many phasing or spatial effects. It also
explains why parallel processing paths with different latency can sound hollow when
recombined.

## Polarity is not arbitrary phase

Reversing polarity multiplies the entire waveform by -1, equivalent to 180° for every
sinusoidal component. A general time shift does not apply the same phase angle at every
frequency.

Use **polarity inversion** and **phase shift** as distinct terms.

## PatchLab experiments

### Observe a low-pass filter

1. Feed broadband noise into SVF.
2. Observe it with Analyzer.
3. Lower cutoff and predict which region will fall.
4. Raise resonance carefully and observe the region near cutoff.

### Create a comb response

1. Split one signal into a dry branch and a SampleDelay branch.
2. Recombine with Add at conservative gain.
3. Change the delay by one sample at a time.
4. Observe the notches move and compare against the audible change.

### Test mono compatibility

1. Create a stereo difference with Width, Pan, or a short delay.
2. Observe Goniometer and Correlation.
3. Recombine or monitor in mono.
4. Identify which apparent width depends on cancellation.

The purpose is not to avoid phase interaction. It is to make it intentional.
