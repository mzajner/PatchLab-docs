A **Container** is a block whose interior is another patch. It lets you work at two
levels at once: a detailed implementation inside and a concise module outside.

## Why use a container?

Use a container when a group of blocks forms one concept:

- a compressor made from detection, gain-computation, VCA, and makeup stages;
- a tape echo with delay, feedback filtering, saturation, and modulation;
- one voice of a synthesizer; or
- a reusable routing or measurement subsystem.

Grouping is not only visual cleanup. A good container gives the subsystem a stable
purpose, defined inputs and outputs, a readable internal flow, and a small intentional
control surface.

## Enter and leave

Double-click a Container header to enter its graph. Use the breadcrumb or press Escape
to move back to its parent.

Inside a container, the endpoint labels change to **PORTS IN** and **PORTS OUT**. They
represent signals crossing the container boundary.

## Expose a macro

Inside a container, right-click a parameter row and choose **Add as macro**. PatchLab
creates a control on the parent-facing Container block.

A container supports up to eight macro slots. A macro inherits the target parameter's
range and units, and can be renamed to express musical intent.

Use **Tone**, **Motion**, or **Space** when those are the intended decisions. Use the
implementation name—such as **LP2 Cutoff**—when the technical mechanism is itself the
public concept. Naming is interface design.

## One macro, multiple targets

A macro can address multiple interior parameters. Each target can have its own range,
response curve, inversion, and display name.

This makes a macro more than a remote knob. It can coordinate a designed transition:
for example, increasing “Intensity” might raise drive, reduce makeup gain, and shift a
filter at different rates.

Test the entire travel. A useful midpoint does not guarantee safe or musical behavior
at either endpoint.

## Design the boundary before hiding the graph

A container is ready to reuse when you can answer:

- What enters and leaves?
- What does it promise to do?
- Which controls should a musician see?
- Which interior details remain intentionally hidden?
- What are its gain, latency, and feedback implications?
- How will someone verify that it is working?

If those answers are unclear, nesting the graph may make it harder—not easier—to
understand.

## Structural variants

Oversample and Poly also contain graphs, but their boundaries change evaluation:

- **Oversample** is appropriate around nonlinear or otherwise alias-sensitive
  processing when the measured benefit justifies the CPU and latency cost.
- **Poly** is appropriate around a note-driven voice that must be instantiated several
  times for chords or overlapping notes.

Do not use either as a decorative grouping tool. Use a plain Container when the goal
is modular organization.
